## Open Analysis

### 1. How should microgravity benchmark data be translated into AM melt-pool process maps?

Directional solidification studies report temperature gradient, interface velocity, cooling rate, undercooling and grain structure. AM studies report laser power, scan speed, feed rate, bead geometry, remelt depth and thermal cycling. The open problem is how to connect these variable sets without losing the physics of CET, solute redistribution and residual flow.

Why it matters: this is the bridge between mature microgravity solidification science and practical in-space metal manufacturing.

### 2. Which residual flow mechanism dominates after buoyancy is suppressed?

Microgravity suppresses buoyancy-driven convection and grain sedimentation, but does not remove Marangoni convection, shrinkage feeding, electromagnetic stirring, surface-pore flow, residual acceleration or melt-pool recoil effects. These mechanisms may dominate different alloy/process combinations.

Why it matters: in-space manufacturing process control depends on the dominant residual mechanism, not on the label "microgravity."

### 3. Which alloy family should anchor the PhD?

Al-Cu and Al-Si have the strongest benchmark literature and are excellent for CET, dendrite growth and macrosegregation. Ti64, TiAl, stainless steel and Ni superalloys are more directly relevant to space hardware and AM qualification. Metallic glasses and refractory eutectics offer distinctive microgravity opportunities through undercooling and phase selection.

Why it matters: the anchor alloy determines whether the thesis is mainly a fundamental solidification study or an in-space manufacturing qualification study.

### 4. Can process monitoring predict final space-made metal quality?

Current orbital metal printing demonstrations still rely heavily on returned sample analysis. A future manufacturing system needs in situ indicators that correlate with grain morphology, porosity, segregation, residual stress and mechanical properties.

Why it matters: autonomous in-space manufacturing will need quality evidence without routine sample return.

### 5. How do lunar-g and Mars-g differ from microgravity?

Most benchmark studies compare 1g with microgravity, but Moon and Mars manufacturing will occur at partial gravity. The scaling between microgravity, lunar-g, Mars-g, 1g and hypergravity remains underdeveloped for AM-like melt pools.

Why it matters: a PhD framed only around microgravity may not transfer cleanly to surface manufacturing on the Moon or Mars.
